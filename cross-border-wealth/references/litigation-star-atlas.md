## System 01 · Valuation Discounts
**Estate of Warne v. Commissioner**
T.C. Memo. 2021-17 · U.S. Tax Court · Feb 18, 2021
`United States` `Estate Tax` `Control / Marketability Discount` `Charitable Deduction`

### Briefing
Miriam Warne's family trust held majority interests in five California real-estate LLCs. When she died, the IRS challenged the estate's valuation of those majority interests and cut a large charitable deduction claimed where one wholly-owned entity (Royal Gardens) was split between a private foundation and a church.

### Analysis
Two threads. First, the Tax Court accepted that even a majority — but less-than-100% — interest can attract discounts for lack of control and lack of marketability, yet it rejected the estate expert's speculative "litigation risk" justification for an aggressive control discount and set its own figure. Second, and more dangerous for planners: when a 100%-owned entity is carved into separate gifts to multiple charities, each charitable gift is valued by what the recipient receives — a discounted minority slice — while the estate is still taxed on the full controlling value. That mismatch leaves value exposed to estate tax even though the whole entity went to charity.

### Reading Guide
Read for the discipline of consistent discounting — uneven discounts hand the IRS an opening — and for the charitable "split-gift" valuation trap. The drafting lesson: give a controlling interest to a single charity rather than fragmenting it. Pair with System 02 (Nelson) on tiered discounts.

### Sources
- [Full opinion (PDF)](https://www.ustaxcourt.gov/USTCInOP/OpinionViewer.aspx?ID=12580) — primary
- [Leagle case page](https://www.leagle.com/decision/intco20210218i58) — primary
- [Withum: Warne & Nelson analysis](https://www.withum.com/resources/) — commentary

## System 02 · Tiered Discounts
**Nelson v. Commissioner**
T.C. Memo. 2020-81 · U.S. Tax Court · Jun 10, 2020
`United States` `Gift Tax` `Tiered / Layered Discounts` `Defined-Value Clause`

### Briefing
Mr. and Mrs. Nelson transferred limited-partnership interests in Longspar — which itself held a minority stake in a holding company owning operating subsidiaries — partly by gift and partly by sale, using "defined value" language meant to fix the dollar amount transferred.

### Analysis
The clause failed on a technicality. Because it fixed the interest by reference to an appraiser's later valuation rather than the value "as finally determined for federal gift tax purposes," the court held the Nelsons had transferred fixed percentage interests, not fixed dollar amounts. But the substance was a win. The court allowed multi-tiered discounts for lack of control and marketability across the stacked entity layers — and the IRS did not even contest the concept — producing roughly a 60% aggregate discount.

### Reading Guide
Read for the exact wording a defined-value clause needs (compare Wandry) and for when discounts may legitimately be layered tier by tier — each entity analysed on its own characteristics. The companion to System 01.

### Sources
- [Full opinion · CourtListener](https://www.courtlistener.com/opinion/4787831/nelson-v-commissioner/) — primary
- [Bessemer Trust summary (PDF)](https://www.bessemertrust.com/) — commentary
- [Briefly Taxing case note](https://brieflytaxing.com/) — commentary

## System 03 · No-Contest Clauses
**Carlson v. Colangelo**
2025 NY Slip Op 02264 · NY Court of Appeals · Apr 17, 2025
`United States` `New York` `In Terrorem / No-Contest Clause` `4-3 Decision`

### Briefing
A decedent's revocable trust left a house and an income stream to his late-life partner, Kristine Carlson, and his LLC interest to his daughter, Crissy Colangelo — who was also trustee. The trust carried a broad in terrorem (no-contest) clause. Carlson sued to obtain her bequests; the lower courts held she had triggered the clause and forfeited everything.

### Analysis
New York's highest court reversed, 4–3. A lawsuit that seeks to enforce and construe a trust as written and intended — rather than to set aside, nullify, or attack the validity of the instrument — does not trigger a no-contest clause, even where the beneficiary advances a contested reading. No-contest clauses are strictly construed and forfeiture is disfavoured. A lengthy dissent argued that challenging the grantor's intended distribution should be enough to forfeit.

### Reading Guide
Read the line the majority draws between "enforce / construe" and "contest / nullify," then read the dissent for how fragile that line is. Essential both for drafting clauses and for advising a beneficiary on whether a claim is safe to file.

### Sources
- [Official NY Reporter slip op](https://www.nycourts.gov/reporter/3dseries/2025/2025_02264.htm) — primary
- [CourtListener](https://www.courtlistener.com/opinion/10380494/carlson-v-colangelo/) — primary

## System 04 · Trustee Fidelity
**Credit Suisse Trust Ltd v. Ivanishvili**
[2024] SGCA(I) 5 · Singapore Court of Appeal · Jul 2024
`Singapore` `Trustee Good Faith` `Fiduciary Duty` `US$1.1bn Trust`

### Briefing
Credit Suisse Trust was trustee of the Mandalay Trust — assets exceeding US$1.1 billion — for former Georgian Prime Minister Bidzina Ivanishvili and his family. Between 2006 and 2015 a Credit Suisse relationship manager, later criminally convicted, defrauded and misappropriated trust assets.

### Analysis
The Singapore Court of Appeal affirmed liability and clarified the trustee's fiduciary duty of good faith and how it interacts with the duty to safeguard trust assets. Tortious and fiduciary duties are conceptually distinct but can be owed and breached by the same party; the trustee had preferred the bank's commercial interests over the beneficiaries'. Damages were measured as the gap between the actual portfolio outcome and a hypothetical, properly-managed portfolio.

### Reading Guide
Read for the taxonomy of a trustee's duties — fiduciary good faith versus tortious care — and for the "hypothetical properly-managed portfolio" damages measure. A cornerstone for trustee-liability analysis across common-law jurisdictions.

- [SG Judiciary / SMU case brief](https://www.judiciary.gov.sg/) — commentary

## System 05 · Division 7A
**Commissioner of Taxation v. Bendel**
[2025] FCAFC 15 · Full Federal Court of Australia · Feb 19, 2025
`Australia` `Family Trusts` `Unpaid Present Entitlements` `Under High Court Appeal`

### Briefing
A discretionary trust made a corporate beneficiary presently entitled to roughly A$1.66m of income across 2013–2016, but the entitlement was never paid out — an "unpaid present entitlement" (UPE). For more than fifteen years the ATO had treated such unpaid UPEs as loans under Division 7A.

### Analysis
The Full Federal Court unanimously held that a UPE is not a "loan" within section 109D(3). Reading the statute closely, a loan requires an obligation to repay money advanced, whereas a UPE is only an obligation to pay an entitlement. The decision overturned a long-standing ATO position.

**Status:** ATO obtained special leave to appeal to the High Court of Australia (granted June 2025). Until resolved, the ATO continues to administer under its existing published view.

## System 06 · State Aid
**European Commission v. Ireland and Apple**
Case C-465/20 P · CJEU (Grand Chamber) · Sep 10, 2024
`European Union` `Unlawful State Aid` `€13bn Recovery` `Final Judgment`

### Briefing
Two Irish tax rulings (1991 and 2007) approved how profits were attributed to the Irish branches of Apple Sales International and Apple Operations Europe. In 2016 the Commission found this amounted to unlawful state aid of about €13 billion. The General Court annulled that decision in 2020; the Commission appealed.

### Analysis
The CJEU set aside the General Court's judgment and confirmed the Commission's 2016 decision: Ireland granted Apple unlawful aid. The Court held the IP licences and related non-US profits should have been allocated to the Irish branches, and that the rulings conferred a selective advantage compared with stand-alone companies taxed on arm's-length profits. Member States keep competence over direct taxation — but must exercise it consistently with EU law.

### Reading Guide
Read for the state-aid selectivity test — reference system, derogation, justification — and the arm's-length reasoning applied to branch profit attribution. A landmark that partly redeemed the Commission after earlier losses.

## System 07 · Social Licence
**Starbucks UK — Transfer Pricing & Public Pressure**
United Kingdom · 2012–2013 · Reuters investigation + Public Accounts Committee
`United Kingdom` `Transfer Pricing` `Reputational Pressure` `Not a Court Ruling`

### Briefing
A 2012 Reuters investigation and UK Public Accounts Committee hearings revealed that Starbucks' UK business had reported losses and paid almost no UK corporation tax for years despite very large sales — using royalty payments to a Netherlands entity, interest on intercompany loans, and coffee beans bought through a Swiss subsidiary at a markup.

### Analysis
No transfer-pricing court judgment. The episode matters as a study in reputational and political pressure substituting for litigation: facing consumer boycott threats, Starbucks voluntarily agreed to pay roughly £20 million in additional UK corporation tax over 2013–2014 — regardless of strict legal liability. It helped catalyse OECD BEPS and the UK's Diverted Profits Tax (2015).

**Classification:** Public-policy episode, not a reported decision.

### Sources
- [UK Public Accounts Committee](https://committees.parliament.uk/) — official
- [Reuters special report](https://www.reuters.com/) — reporting

## System 08 · Marketing Hubs
**Rio Tinto — ATO Singapore Marketing Hub Settlement**
Australia · Jul 20, 2022 · ATO + IRAS settlement
`Australia` `Transfer Pricing` `Singapore Marketing Hub` `~A$1bn Settlement`

### Briefing
The ATO challenged the pricing of iron-ore and aluminium marketing routed through Rio Tinto's Singapore "commercial centre," plus a separate intragroup-dividend financing matter.

### Analysis
In July 2022 Rio Tinto settled, with total payments approaching A$1 billion — including A$613m of additional tax for 2010–2021. Crucially, Rio also reached a corresponding agreement with Singapore's IRAS to avoid double taxation, and secured certainty through 2026.

**Classification:** Negotiated settlement, not a judgment.

## System 09 · Intra-Group Debt
**HMRC v. BlackRock Holdco 5 LLC**
[2022] UKUT 199 (TCC) · Upper Tribunal · Jul 19, 2022
`United Kingdom` `Loan Relationships` `Transfer Pricing` `Unallowable Purpose`

### Briefing
As part of BlackRock's 2009 acquisition of Barclays Global Investors, a UK-resident Delaware LLC ("LLC5") issued about US$4 billion of loan notes and claimed loan-relationship interest deductions over six years.

### Analysis
The Upper Tribunal disallowed the deductions on two independent grounds. Transfer pricing: an arm's-length lender would not have made a US$4bn loan without covenants from third parties that were not in fact present. Unallowable purpose: a main purpose of the loans was securing a tax advantage.

**Court of Appeal ([2024] EWCA Civ 330, Apr 11, 2024):** The CA (styled BlackRock HoldCo 5, LLC v HMRC on appeal) **reversed** the UT on transfer pricing — finding no TP restriction applied — but **upheld** the disallowance on unallowable purpose grounds. Net result: deductions still disallowed, but on only one ground.

## System 10 · Mandatory Disclosure
**Belgian Association of Tax Lawyers and Others v. EU**
Case C-623/22 · CJEU (Second Chamber) · Jul 29, 2024
`European Union` `DAC6 Validity` `Charter of Fundamental Rights` `Legal Privilege`

### Briefing
The Belgian Constitutional Court referred five questions to the CJEU on the validity of DAC6 — the EU's mandatory-disclosure regime — challenged under the EU Charter of Fundamental Rights.

### Analysis
The CJEU upheld DAC6's validity. The reporting obligation interferes with the right to private life, but the interference is justified and proportionate. Key qualification: the legal-professional-privilege waiver extends only to lawyers, not to all professionals bound by privilege under national law.

## System 11 · Fund Architecture
**Zhong Shan Strategic Fund v. RG Strategy Fund VCC**
[2025] SGHC 174 · Singapore High Court · 2025
`Singapore` `Variable Capital Company` `Sub-Fund Liquidation` `First Guidance`

### Briefing
Zhong Shan — a Cayman fund holding 53.6% of the RG Asset-Backed Investment Fund I ("ABIFI") sub-fund within the RG Strategy Fund VCC umbrella — applied to wind up that sub-fund. First detailed Singapore guidance on liquidating a VCC sub-fund.

### Analysis
The court clarified the procedure: a sub-fund may be wound up "as if it were a legal person," but the application must name the VCC itself as defendant. The court worked through the statutory grounds and discretionary factors to decline a winding-up order.

## System 12 · Trustee Duty & Arbitration
**Pinnacle Trust Company LLC v. McTaggart**
2013-CA-01920-SCT · Supreme Court of Mississippi · 2014
`United States` `Mississippi` `Fiduciary Duty` `Arbitration of Non-Signatory`

### Briefing
Beneficiaries of a family trust sued the former trustee for breach of fiduciary duty, alleging a loss exceeding US$1.5 million. The defendants moved to compel arbitration under a clause in a wealth-management agreement the beneficiaries had never signed.

### Analysis
The Mississippi Supreme Court held the beneficiaries were not bound. They had not signed the agreement; the agreement itself excluded non-signatories; and their claim arose from statutory fiduciary duty, not from the contract.

## System 13 · Trust Arbitration
**McArthur v. McArthur**
224 Cal.App.4th 651 · California Court of Appeal (1st Dist.) · Mar 11, 2014
`United States` `California` `Arbitration Clause in a Trust` `Issue of First Impression`

### Briefing
A 2011 trust amendment gave one daughter a larger share, made her co-trustee, and added a "Christian Dispute Resolution" arbitration clause. After the settlor died, another daughter challenged the amendment for undue influence and lack of capacity.

### Analysis
The Court of Appeal — facing an issue of first impression in California — affirmed denial of arbitration. The plaintiff never signed the arbitration clause; California's Probate Code contains no authority for pre-dispute trust arbitration; and a beneficiary contesting a trust's validity has not consented as a third-party beneficiary. Compare: Texas (Rachal v. Reitz) enforced such a clause; Arizona (Schoneberger) did not.

## System 14 · Litigation Funding
**DNQ v. DNR**
[2025] SGHC 152 · Singapore High Court (General Division) · Aug 7, 2025
`Singapore` `Third-Party Litigation Funding` `Maintenance & Champerty` `First of its Kind`

### Briefing
A UK citizen sought to enforce roughly £31.2 million of judgment debts from UK family-court proceedings. To fund the enforcement, she entered a third-party funding agreement (up to US$6 million; ~56% of proceeds to the funder).

### Analysis
The High Court upheld the funding agreement — the first reported Singapore decision recognising third-party litigation funding outside insolvency and arbitration. Applying the two-part test from Re Fan Kow Hin: the funder has a legitimate interest, the claimant retains control, and there is no prejudice to the administration of justice.

## System 15 · The Hallucination Belt
**AI Citation Sanctions Cases**
Multi-jurisdiction · 2023–2026 · led by Mata v. Avianca (S.D.N.Y. 2023)
`Multi-Jurisdiction` `AI-Fabricated Citations` `Professional Responsibility` `Sanctions`

### Briefing
A growing line of decisions sanctioning lawyers for filing briefs with fabricated, AI-"hallucinated" case citations. The foundational case is Mata v. Avianca, Inc. (S.D.N.Y. 2023), where Judge Castel sanctioned two attorneys under Rule 11 for ChatGPT-generated fake precedents.

### Analysis
Since 2023, hundreds of incidents across U.S. and international courts. Courts rely on Rule 11 and inherent authority; sanctions range from monetary fines to mandatory disclosure orders. The ABA issued Formal Opinion 512 on generative AI on July 29, 2024, covering competence, confidentiality, candor, and fees.

### Reading Guide
This applies directly to anyone using a model for legal research: treat AI output as a lead, never as authority. Every citation on this very page is meant to be opened and checked.
